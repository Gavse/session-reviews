This session dismantled the "AI as equalizer" narrative by exposing six methodological limits of the Cruces study, then rebuilt the framework from scratch. The key move: problems aren't just "tasks AI can do" — they're closed, closed-delayed, or open, and each type predicts a different human-AI dynamic. Key Takeaways — the 8 ideas that change how you read AI research
  1. The "75% gap closure" applies only to closed problems with fictional stakes — change any condition and the finding shrinks.
  2. Three problem types (closed / closed-delayed / open) predict AI's equalizer effect better than any single study variable.
  3. Any complex task splits into four layers (goals, variables, weights, reframing) — AI fits differently in each.
  4. The "GenAI Wall" at Layer 2: AI generates impressive variable lists, but novices can't evaluate which are valid vs. spurious.
  5. Most AI-vs-human studies measure efficiency, not effectiveness — the evidence base systematically overstates standalone AI value.
  6. Task-level equalization coexists with growing expert premium because remaining human tasks shift toward open problems.
  7. Data degradation and environmental flux continuously reopen "closed" problems — today's automation is tomorrow's open question.
  8. Prediction feels open but is functionally closed-delayed — three converging literatures confirm algorithms beat human intuition here.
