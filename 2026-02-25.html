Mini case

A mid-size consulting firm ran a 6-week AI pilot with 40 consultants. Junior analysts (1-3 years experience) with GPT-4 produced client memos in half the usual time — blind quality ratings hit "adequate" consistently, closing the gap with seniors. The CEO presented this at the board as proof AI is "the great equalizer." Two months later, Q1 results came in: senior partners' AI-assisted clients renewed at 94%; junior-led AI-assisted accounts renewed at 61%. The board now wants to understand what went wrong with the pilot's conclusion. As you read this review, build the explanation.

1. Cruces et al. — Seven Limitations

The "75% education gap closure" is qualified by seven limitations suggesting the equalizer finding may be an artifact of task structure.

LimitationWhy it matters
General taskLowers the floor, biases toward equalizer
Value curve unknownLikely diminishing returns
Construct validityRubric = surface quality, not economic value
Fictional stakesSatisficing, not maximizing
One-shot designCan't measure learning or adaptation

Which limitation is most damaging to generalizing the finding? Think first.

Closed problem structure. Component breakdown shows AI closed the gap on problem identification, solution generation, and organization — all closed subtasks. The equalizer finding is about task type, not a general truth about AI and skill gaps.

2. Value Curve Typology — Four Types
Sentence Stem

The binary concave/convex distinction misses tasks where value jumps discontinuously at discrete quality thresholds rather than following a smooth curve.

TypeShapeGap prediction
Smooth concaveGradually diminishingEqualizer
Smooth convexSuperlinearExpert advantage
Step functionDiscrete pass/failDepends which threshold AI crosses
Plateau-jumpBands with jumpsEqualizes within band; experts jump between
Why does the plateau-jump type explain more than the others? Think first.

It captures the satisficing dynamic: participants stop at the first "good enough" plateau. AI lifts novices there — equalizer. But higher bands exist with discrete value jumps. Expert advantage lives above the first plateau, invisible to study designs with low-stakes motivation.

Why — Why would lab motivation systematically bias which finding a study produces?
3. Motivation: Satisficing Pre-Selects Findings

Fictional stakes → satisficing (converge at first plateau). Real stakes → maximizing (push past "good enough"). Both groups stop at the same place when satisficing — where AI already gets everyone. The equalizer finding is partly an artifact of this convergence.

Does this bias affect ALL lab RCTs on AI-collaboration? Consider first.

If correct: Cruces, Noy & Zhang, Dell'Acqua, Peng — all share this bias. No study with real career/pay stakes exists yet. The equalizer finding may not survive outside the lab.

Connection

Sections 2 + 3: How does the value curve type determine whether the satisficing bias matters? In smooth concave tasks, the first plateau IS the ceiling — bias doesn't matter. In plateau-jump tasks, it hides expert advantage in higher bands.

Connection — The Vaccaro meta showed human+AI is worse when AI > human (g=-0.54). How might this connect to whether a problem is "closed" vs. "open"?

8. Efficiency vs. Effectiveness
Sentence Stem

Most studies measure efficiency (task faster/cheaper) → favors AI. Few measure effectiveness (right thing in changing context) → favors human+AI because "right thing" keeps shifting.

Prediction — Can the equalizer finding at task level coexist with growing inequality at labor market level? How?
9. Temporal Trajectory — The Composition Paradox
HorizonGap effect
NowEqualizer on closed; expert advantage on open
Near (1-3y)Equalizer expands, BUT human tasks skew open
Medium (3-10y)Expert premium grows for what's left
Far (10y+)Unknown — depends if AI enters open domains
The paradox: how can both be true simultaneously?

As AI automates closed tasks, the distribution of human work shifts toward open tasks — where expert advantage is strongest. Equalizer within each task type + composition shift toward expert-favoring tasks = growing inequality at market level.

10. GenAI Brain + Algorithm Tools

For structured prediction, traditional ML (XGBoost, random forests) still wins at scale. GenAI reasons about which model, how to frame, what features — then delegates to specialized algorithms.

Maps to four layers: GenAI handles 1-2, algorithms handle 3, humans contribute 4.
